# From 7 tools to 1 conversation: a real migration story

This is the story of a consultant I will call Dana. She is a composite of several real clients, but the numbers are exact. She came in running seven paid tools. Seven logins. Seven monthly invoices. She left running one. This is a consolidate business tools migration guide based on what actually happened during her [7-Day Build](/blog/managed-operations/7-day-build-explained).

I am not going to pitch you on why consolidation matters. I wrote that [already](/blog/tool-consolidation/replace-saas-stack-guide). This is the day-by-day walkthrough of what it looks like when someone actually does it.

---

## What Dana was running before

Seven tools. $412 per month. Around 18 hours per week in manual admin work.

Here is the full stack, what she paid, and what she actually used each tool for:

| Tool | Monthly Cost | What Dana Actually Did With It |
|---|---|---|
| Calendly Teams | $16 | Sent booking links. One reminder email. |
| FreshBooks Plus | $30 | Sent invoices. Checked who paid. |
| HoneyBook Essentials | $59 | Contracts and intake forms. Nothing else. |
| Mailchimp Standard | $20 | Monthly newsletter. Skipped it half the time. |
| Google Sheets | Free | Client tracker. Revenue tracker. The real source of truth. |
| Asana Premium | $11 | Onboarding checklists. Task reminders she ignored. |
| Slack Pro | $8.75 | Reminders to herself. Message history search. |

**Total: $144.75/mo in subscriptions.** But the real number was $412/mo when you added the per-contact Mailchimp overage, Calendly's second user seat for her VA, the FreshBooks Premium plan she upgraded to after hitting 50 clients, and the HoneyBook annual plan she forgot auto-renewed at $59/mo.

The [subscription audit](/blog/tool-consolidation/saas-subscription-audit) is always where the surprises live.

---

## Day 1: The diagnosis

We mapped every tool to every task. Not features. Tasks. What Dana actually did on a Monday morning, a Wednesday afternoon, a Friday wrap-up.

The result was uncomfortable. She used maybe 15 percent of Calendly's features. Maybe 20 percent of FreshBooks. HoneyBook was the worst. She was paying $59/mo for contracts and an intake form. That was it. The CRM, the pipeline view, the project tracking, the automations. All untouched.

She said something I hear a lot: "I always meant to set those up."

Nobody does.

---

## Day 2: Building the foundation

We started with the two things every service business needs working before anything else: scheduling and invoicing.

Dana's Calendly had three booking types. We rebuilt them as routines in Arca. Same availability windows. Same buffer times. Same reminder sequence. But now when someone books, the system already knows who they are. Their intake answers, their invoice history, their last session notes. All connected. No tab switching.

Her FreshBooks invoices followed a pattern. 80 percent of them were the same three line items with the same payment terms. We turned those into [blueprints](/blog/tool-consolidation/replace-saas-stack-guide). Invoice gets generated automatically after the session. Payment reminder goes out on day 3 and day 7. She never touches it unless something is unusual.

---

## Day 3: Client management and contracts

This was the HoneyBook replacement. Dana's contracts were standard. Two versions: one for individual coaching, one for corporate workshops. We migrated both.

The difference now is what happens after someone signs. In HoneyBook, signing a contract was a dead end. Dana had to manually create the client in FreshBooks, add them to her Google Sheet, tag them in Mailchimp, and create an onboarding checklist in Asana. Five tools touched by one event.

In Arca, signing triggers everything. Client record created. First invoice scheduled. Onboarding sequence started. Welcome email sent. Dana does not touch any of it.

---

## Day 4: Email and follow-ups

Mailchimp was handling Dana's newsletter and two automated sequences: a welcome series for new subscribers and a re-engagement series for past clients.

The newsletter was the one thing Dana cared about keeping. She liked writing it. She just hated the 45-minute process of formatting it in Mailchimp, segmenting the list, previewing on mobile, and scheduling it.

Now she writes the newsletter in a chat message. Arca handles formatting, segmentation, and send. Total time: the time it takes to write. Nothing more.

The automated sequences moved over directly. Same copy. Same timing. But now they are connected to everything else. When a past client opens three emails in a row, that shows up in Dana's morning briefing. Not buried in Mailchimp analytics she never checks.

---

## Day 5: The spreadsheet

Every consultant has one. The Google Sheet that holds the real truth about the business. Client status. Revenue by month. Pipeline. Notes that do not fit anywhere else.

Dana's had 14 columns and 230 rows. It was the most important tool in her stack, and it was free.

We did not just migrate the data. We made it unnecessary. Every column in that spreadsheet mapped to information that Arca already tracks. Client status updates automatically based on activity. Revenue calculates from actual invoices. Pipeline reflects real conversations, not manual entries.

Dana still checks the numbers. She just does not have to maintain them.

---

## Day 6: Notifications and reminders

This was the Slack and Asana replacement. Dana used Slack mostly as a reminder system. "Remind me to follow up with Sarah on Thursday." "Remind me to send the proposal by end of day." She used Asana for onboarding checklists and recurring task lists.

Both of these are now part of the morning briefing. Every day, Dana gets a summary: who booked, who paid, who needs follow-up, what is due. Not in a separate app. In the same conversation where she does everything else.

The workers-switch-apps-33-times-per-day statistic suddenly makes sense when you watch someone stop doing it.

---

## Day 7: Go-live and the reaction

We ran both systems in parallel for the last day. Old stack still active. Arca handling everything.

By noon, Dana had not opened a single one of her old tools. Not because we told her not to. Because there was no reason to. Everything was happening in one place.

Her exact words at the end of day 7: "I feel stupid for not doing this sooner."

She is not stupid. She was doing what everyone does. Adding one tool at a time, each solving one problem, until the stack itself became the problem.

---

## The numbers after 30 days

Here is where Dana landed one month after the migration:

| Metric | Before | After |
|---|---|---|
| Monthly tool cost | $412 | $97 (Arca Foundation) |
| Weekly admin hours | 18 | 4 |
| Tools with active logins | 7 | 1 |
| Client onboarding time | 45 min | 8 min |
| Invoice follow-up | Manual, often late | Automatic, same day |

$315/mo saved. 14 hours/week recovered. That is $3,780/year back in her pocket and 728 hours back in her calendar.

But the number Dana talks about most is not the money. It is the 45-minute onboarding that became 8 minutes. That was the moment consolidation stopped being theoretical and became visceral.

## What this means for your stack

Dana's story is specific. Your stack will be different. Maybe you do not use HoneyBook. Maybe you have Dubsado instead. Maybe your spreadsheet has 40 columns instead of 14.

The pattern is always the same. Too many tools. Not enough of each tool used. And hours every week spent being the human glue between systems that should be talking to each other.

The 7-Day Build is not magic. It is just what happens when someone finally decides to stop paying for seven tools and using 20 percent of each.

## FAQ

**Q: How long does a typical tool migration take?**
A: DIY migrations take 20-40 hours over 2-6 weeks. Done-for-you setup (like the 7-Day Build) takes 3-4 hours of your time over 7 days.

**Q: What happens to my existing client data during migration?**
A: Client records, invoice history, and scheduled content transfer to the new system. Most consultants run parallel systems for 1 week during transition.

**Q: How much money can I save by consolidating tools?**
A: The average consultant saves $300-500/month in subscriptions. Dana's story shows $315/mo saved plus 14 hours/week recovered.

**Q: Do I need to cancel all my tools at once?**
A: No. Pause tools during the 7-day transition. Cancel only after confirming the new system handles everything. Keep niche/industry-specific tools.
