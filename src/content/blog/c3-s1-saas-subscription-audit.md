Most founders I talk to can't tell me their total SaaS spend within $100. That's not a guess. I've asked. A proper SaaS subscription cost small business audit usually reveals $300 to $500 per month spread across five to seven tools. And the dollar amount is only half the problem.

The other half is what those tools do to your attention.

## What a typical small business stack actually costs

A common setup looks something like this. Calendly at $16 per month. FreshBooks at $30. HoneyBook somewhere between $49 and $80. Mailchimp at $20. A project management tool at $10 to $15. That puts you at $125 to $161 per month before you add any one-off purchases or annual renewals you forgot about.

Statista's 2024 data says the average small business uses 42 SaaS applications. You probably think you're nowhere near that. Pull your credit card statement and count. You'll surprise yourself.

Most SMBs spend 6 to 12 percent of revenue on software subscriptions. And according to Gartner, roughly 30 percent of that spend is "toxic," meaning it goes to tools that are redundant, underused, or outright forgotten.

## How to run your own subscription audit

Start with your bank and credit card statements from the last 90 days. Every recurring charge gets a line in a spreadsheet. Here's what to capture for each tool.

### The audit columns

**Tool name.** Self-explanatory.

**Monthly cost.** Convert annual plans to monthly. A $228 annual subscription is $19 per month, not free.

**What you actually use it for.** Not what the tool does. What you do with it. Be specific. "Send invoices" is better than "financial management."

**Feature usage percentage.** Estimate honestly. If you use three features out of fifteen, that's 20 percent. Pendo's research shows that 80 percent of SaaS features are rarely or never used. You're not the exception.

**Overlap with other tools.** Does this tool do something another tool already does? Flag it.

**Last login date.** Check your browser history or the tool's own activity log. If you haven't logged in within 30 days, that's a signal.

### Benchmarks by business type

**Service businesses (consultants, coaches, agencies):** Your core needs are scheduling, invoicing, client communication, and a CRM. Four functions. If you're paying for more than four tools, something overlaps.

**Product businesses (e-commerce, DTC):** Core needs include inventory, order management, email marketing, and analytics. Same rule applies. Count the functions, then count the tools.

**Freelancers and solopreneurs:** You need invoicing, scheduling, and a pipeline tracker. Three functions. Three tools maximum. Anything beyond that is comfort spending.

## The cost you're not counting

Here's where the audit gets interesting. Every tool in your stack carries a cognitive cost that never shows up on an invoice.

Research from UC Irvine found it takes 23 minutes and 15 seconds to regain full focus after switching tasks. Lokalise's 2025 report says workers switch between applications 33 times per day and lose 51 minutes per week to tool fatigue alone.

That's not an abstract number. That's almost an hour every week where you're not selling, not delivering, not building. You're clicking between tabs and trying to remember where you saw that client note.

When I ran my own audit before building Arca, the dollar cost surprised me. But the time cost shocked me. I was spending 30 to 40 minutes every morning just syncing information between tools that should have been talking to each other.

## What to do with your audit results

Once you have your spreadsheet, sort by feature usage percentage. The tools below 30 percent usage are candidates for elimination or [consolidation](/blog/c3-pillar-replace-saas-stack/).

Look for the tools you open every morning just to check one thing. That's the [six-tabs-every-morning problem](/blog/c3-s2-six-open-tabs-every-morning/). Each of those partial checks adds a context switch to your day.

Then look at total spend. Compare it to what a single platform could do for the same functions. Not an [all-in-one tool that tries to do everything](/blog/c3-s3-why-all-in-one-tools-fail/). A system that's configured for the specific things you actually do.

## The spreadsheet framework

Build your audit spreadsheet with these columns and fill in every recurring charge.

| Tool | Monthly Cost | Function | Feature Usage % | Overlap? | Last Login |
|------|-------------|----------|-----------------|----------|------------|
| Example: Calendly | $16 | Scheduling | 40% | Partial (CRM has scheduling) | Yesterday |

Run this quarterly. SaaS creep is real and it's quiet. A free trial converts to a paid plan. A team member signs up for something without telling you. Three months later, you're paying for a tool nobody uses.

## From audit to action

The point of this exercise isn't just to cancel a few subscriptions and save $50 a month. It's to see the full picture of how fragmented your operations have become.

Every tool is a login. Every login is a context switch. Every context switch is lost time. The [real operational cost](/blog/c1-s2-operations-cost-mistake/) isn't what you pay for software. It's what you pay in attention.

I built Arca because I got tired of being the human middleware between seven different dashboards. The audit was step one. Seeing the problem clearly was what made the solution obvious.

Run the audit. The numbers will speak for themselves.

## FAQ

**Q: How often should I run a SaaS subscription audit?**
A: Quarterly. SaaS creep is real — free trials convert to paid plans, team members sign up for tools without telling you, and unused subscriptions pile up over time.

**Q: What's a reasonable SaaS budget for a small business?**
A: Most SMBs spend 6-12% of revenue on software. For a $500K/year consultancy, that's $2,500-5,000/month. But 30% of that spend is typically "toxic" — unused or redundant tools.

**Q: What's the biggest cost of too many SaaS tools?**
A: It's not the subscription fees. Workers lose 51 minutes/week to tool-switching (Lokalise 2025), and it takes 23+ minutes to refocus after each context switch (UC Irvine).

**Q: How do I know if a tool is worth keeping?**
A: Check feature usage %. If you use less than 30% of features and haven't logged in for 30+ days, it's a candidate for elimination or consolidation.
