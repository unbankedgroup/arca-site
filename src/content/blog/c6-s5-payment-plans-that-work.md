# Payment Plans That Actually Get Paid

**Quick answer:** Payment plans that get paid have three things: auto-billing (not manual invoices), clear terms (due dates, late fees, what happens if they default), and automated dunning (reminder sequences that escalate). Manual payment collection costs 11 hours/month and 34% of late payments become never-payments.

---

I had a coach come to me last year. She was doing $180K in revenue on paper. Looked great. But she couldn't access $47,000 of it — clients who had signed up for payment plans and stopped paying.

She was sending manual invoices. Waiting for clients to pay. Sending awkward follow-up emails when they didn't. Some clients paid 30 days late. Some never paid. She didn't want to seem "pushy" so she waited.

Here's what I told her: you're not being pushy. You're running a business. And your payment plan structure is inviting people to not pay you.

Payment plans are a tool for accessibility. They let clients work with you who couldn't afford your full price. But if your payment plan depends on clients remembering to pay, you don't have a payment plan. You have a hope-and-pray system.

## The real cost of manual payment collection

Let me show you the math.

**Time spent on manual payment collection:**
- Creating and sending invoices: 15-20 minutes per client per month
- Tracking who has paid: 30-60 minutes per week
- Sending late payment reminders: 20-30 minutes per late client
- Reconciling payments: 30-45 minutes per week

For a coach with 20 clients on payment plans:
- **11 hours per month** just on payment collection
- At $150-200/hour: **$1,650-2,200/month in your time**

But time isn't the real cost. The real cost is revenue that never arrives.

**Payment plan failure rates:**
- 44% of small businesses manually create invoices weekly
- 50% of those invoices are overdue
- 34% of late payments become never-payments
- Average payment is 7-10 days late without auto-billing, 2-3 days with it

For that coach with $47K outstanding? She'll never see about $16,000 of it. Not because her clients are bad. Because her system invited them to forget.

## The anatomy of a payment plan that gets paid

Here's what works.

### 1. Auto-billing, not manual invoices

This is non-negotiable. Your payment processor should charge the client's card automatically on the due date. No invoice to pay. No link to click. No remembering required.

**Auto-billing vs manual invoices:**

| Feature | Auto-billing | Manual Invoice |
|---------|-------------|----------------|
| On-time payment rate | 94%+ | 50-60% |
| Your time per month | <30 min | 11+ hours |
| Late payment rate | 6% | 50% |
| Client experience | Seamless | Friction |

Auto-billing isn't mean. It's what your clients signed up for. They agreed to pay. Make it happen.

### 2. Clear terms upfront

Your payment plan agreement should be explicit about:
- **Total price** — "This program is $6,000"
- **Payment schedule** — "$1,000/month for 6 months" or "$500/month for 12 months"
- **Due date** — "Payments are due on the 1st of each month"
- **Late fee** — "$50 after 5 days, $100 after 10 days"
- **Default terms** — "After 30 days late, access to program is suspended"
- **Refund policy** — "No refunds after first session" or "Pro-rated within first 30 days"

Don't be subtle. Don't be vague. Don't bury it in a 20-page contract.

**Example payment terms:**
```
Payment Plan: $6,000 total
- $2,000 due at signing
- $1,000 due on the 1st of each month for 4 months

Payment Method: Credit card on file, auto-charged
Late Fee: $50 after 5 days, $100 after 10 days
Default: After 30 days late, program access is suspended until payment
```

### 3. Automated dunning sequences

Dunning is the process of recovering failed payments. Your payment processor should handle this automatically.

**Standard dunning sequence:**
- **Day 0:** Payment attempted, successful → receipt sent
- **Day 0 (failed):** Payment failed → "Update your card" email sent
- **Day 3:** Retry payment → if failed, "Please update payment method" email
- **Day 7:** Second retry → "Payment overdue, please update" email + late fee notice
- **Day 14:** Third retry → "Final notice, account will be suspended" email
- **Day 30:** Account suspended → access revoked until payment

This isn't aggressive. This is business. And it happens automatically — no awkward emails from you.

**Data point:** Automated dunning recovers 60-70% of failed payments within 14 days. Manual follow-up recovers about 30%.

### 4. Multiple payment methods

Give clients options:
- Credit/debit card (primary)
- ACH/bank transfer (lower fees, good for large payments)
- PayPal (some clients prefer it)
- Wire transfer (for international clients)

The more friction you add, the more likely they are to "forget" to pay.

### 5. Grace periods with teeth

A grace period is fine. A grace period without consequences is a policy invitation to ignore.

**Good grace period:**
"Payments are due on the 1st. You have a 5-day grace period with no late fee. After day 5, a $50 late fee is applied. After day 10, a $100 late fee is applied. After day 30, your access is suspended."

**Bad grace period:**
"Payments are due on the 1st. Just let me know if you need flexibility!"

One is a policy. One is a suggestion.

## Payment plan structures that work

Here are three structures I've seen succeed.

### Structure 1: Front-loaded

- 30-50% due at signing
- Remainder split over 3-6 months
- Best for: Programs with heavy upfront work

**Why it works:** You're not exposed if they default. You've already collected most of your fee.

### Structure 2: Equal monthly payments

- 10-20% due at signing
- Equal payments for 6-12 months
- Best for: Long-term coaching engagements

**Why it works:** Predictable for client and you. Cash flow is steady.

### Structure 3: Milestone-based

- 25% at signing
- 25% at program midpoint
- 50% split over remaining months
- Best for: Programs with clear milestones

**Why it works:** Payments align with value delivery. Client sees progress before paying more.

## When payment plans backfire

Payment plans don't work for everyone. Here's when to be cautious.

### Red flags:
- Client asks for unusually long payment terms (12+ months for a $3K program)
- Client hesitates on auto-billing ("Can I just pay manually each month?")
- Client has a history of late payments (ask for references)
- Client's financial situation is unstable (they tell you they're between jobs)

### What to do:
- Require larger upfront payment (50%+)
- Shorten the payment term (3 months max)
- Offer a discount for upfront payment ("Pay in full and save 15%")
- Decline the engagement if the risk is too high

You're not a bank. You're a coach. Don't finance clients who can't afford you.

## The conversation about money

Here's the script I give coaches.

**When they ask for payment plans:**
"Absolutely. The full investment is $6,000. We can do $2,000 at signing and $1,000/month for 4 months. All payments are auto-charged to your card on the 1st of each month. Does that work for you?"

Notice what happened:
- You stated the price confidently
- You offered the plan matter-of-factly
- You explained the mechanism (auto-billing)
- You asked if it works (yes/no question)

Don't apologize. Don't hedge. Don't say "I know it's a lot but..."

It's not a lot if it transforms their business. Price is about value, not cost.

## The bottom line

Payment plans are a tool. Used correctly, they make your services accessible while protecting your cash flow. Used poorly, they turn you into a collections agency.

Auto-billing. Clear terms. Automated dunning. No awkward conversations.

Your clients who intend to pay will pay on time. Your clients who don't intend to pay will self-select out. And you'll get paid for the work you're doing.

## FAQ

### What is a payment plan?

A payment plan lets clients pay for services over time instead of upfront. For example: $6,000 program = $2,000 at signing + $1,000/month for 4 months. Auto-billing is essential for payment plans to work.

### Should I charge interest on payment plans?

Most coaches don't charge interest — it's built into the pricing. If your upfront price is $5,000 and payment plan total is $6,000, the extra $1,000 is effectively interest. Be transparent about the total.

### What if a client can't pay?

Your payment plan agreement should specify what happens: late fees, suspension of access, and collection procedures. Stick to your terms. Making exceptions for one client sets a precedent for all clients.

### How many months should I offer?

3-6 months is standard for programs under $10K. 6-12 months works for high-ticket engagements ($15K+). Anything longer and you're financing the client, not coaching them.

### Should I offer a discount for upfront payment?

Yes. 10-15% discount for upfront payment rewards clients who can pay and improves your cash flow. Example: "$6,000 or $5,100 paid in full upfront (15% discount)."
