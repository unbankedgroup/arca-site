# Intake Forms That Actually Tell You Something Useful (Not Just Check Boxes)

A coach showed me her intake form. Thirty-two fields. Name, email, phone, address, birthday, emergency contact, medical history, dietary restrictions, previous coaching experience (drop-down), goals (checkboxes: career, relationships, health, finances, spirituality), and a text box at the bottom labeled "Anything else?"

She asked me why clients rush through it.

Because it's a data entry form pretending to be a conversation. A good client intake form for coaching or consulting should surface the information that actually matters for the first session. Not collect demographic data for a spreadsheet.

**TL;DR:** The best intake forms ask 5-8 open-ended questions that reveal what the client has tried, what's not working, what success looks like, and what they're afraid of. Short forms get thoughtful answers. Long forms get checkbox responses. Design the intake form around what you need to show up prepared for session one, not what a CRM template told you to collect.

## Why most intake forms fail

Two common mistakes.

**Mistake 1: Too many questions.** Thirty-two fields means the client fills it out in "get through this" mode. Short answers. Checked boxes. The minimum effort to reach the submit button. You get data. You don't get insight.

Research from SurveyMonkey shows that completion rates drop by 15-20% for every additional 5 questions beyond 10. Forms with 5-8 open-ended questions get 3x more thoughtful responses than forms with 20+ checkbox fields.

**Mistake 2: Wrong questions.** "What are your goals?" with five checkboxes tells you nothing you don't already know. She checked "career" and "confidence." Great. So did every other client. The checkbox doesn't tell you that she lost a promotion last month and cried in her car for 20 minutes. That's the information that changes the first session.

The intake form should be a bridge between the discovery call and the first session. It takes what the client shared verbally and deepens it in writing. It gives the coach context that makes the first session feel like the second. The client walks in and thinks: "She already gets me."

## The 7 questions that actually matter

These work across coaching niches. Adjust the language for your audience, but the structure applies universally.

### 1. "What made you decide to start coaching right now?"

Not "what are your goals." What made NOW the moment. There's always a trigger. A bad review. A health scare. A birthday. A breakup. A moment of "I can't keep doing this." That trigger tells you more about motivation than any goals checklist.

### 2. "What have you already tried?"

This prevents you from suggesting things they've already done. It shows you their problem-solving style. It tells you whether they're action-oriented or stuck in analysis. A client who's read 12 books and taken 3 courses is different from one who's never had support before.

### 3. "What's not working about how you're currently handling this?"

Specific. Not "what's your biggest challenge?" but "what specifically isn't working?" The specificity forces a real answer. "I can't say no to clients who want discounts" is more useful than "boundaries."

### 4. "What does success look like in 90 days?"

Not "what are your long-term goals." Ninety days. Specific enough to be actionable. Vague enough to be aspirational. "I want to have raised my rates and kept all my clients" is a 90-day picture you can work with. "I want to be happier" is not.

### 5. "What are you afraid might happen in this process?"

This is the question most coaches skip. And it's the most valuable one. Fear drives behavior more than ambition does. If she's afraid of confrontation, that tells you everything about how to approach the work. If she's afraid of failing again, you know the stakes feel personal.

### 6. "Is there anything I should know about how you work best?"

Learning style. Communication preferences. Energy patterns. Some clients need accountability. Some need space. Some want direct feedback. Some need gentle reframing. This question lets the client tell you how to coach them, which is a gift most coaches don't ask for.

### 7. "What question do you wish I'd asked?"

Open door. Some clients have something they need to say but don't have a box for it. This catches it. The answers to this question are often the most revealing on the entire form.

## Design principles

**Keep it under 10 questions.** Seven is ideal. Under 10 is acceptable. Over 10 and completion quality drops significantly.

**Use open text fields, not checkboxes.** Checkboxes collect data. Open fields collect stories. You want stories.

**Set expectations on length.** "This takes about 10 minutes" reduces the intimidation. "Take your time with these" signals that thoughtful answers matter.

**Don't ask what you already know.** If she told you about her business on the discovery call, don't make her repeat it in the form. Reference it instead: "You mentioned on our call that you're running a nutrition practice with 18 clients. Has anything changed since we spoke?"

**Make it part of the sequence.** The intake form should arrive within hours of signing, not days. Clients are most engaged immediately [after the decision](/blog/client-onboarding/first-48-hours-onboarding). Use that momentum. The form is the second step of a [structured onboarding flow](/blog/client-onboarding/client-onboarding-automation-guide), not a standalone task.

## What to do with the answers

The intake form is only as valuable as what happens after submission. The client fills it out. Then what?

**Step 1:** The coach gets notified and reviews the responses before the first session. Not during. Before.

**Step 2:** The coach sends a brief personalized prep note referencing specific answers. "You mentioned that delegation is your biggest struggle right now. I'd like to start there." This is the step that transforms the [onboarding experience](/blog/client-onboarding/automated-welcome-sequence).

**Step 3:** The responses get stored with the client profile so they're accessible throughout the engagement. Session 6, when the client mentions progress on delegation, the coach can reference the original answer.

A [diagnostic approach](/blog/managed-operations/diagnosis-before-prescription) to intake means the form serves the coaching, not just the filing system. Every question should earn its place by making the first session better.

If your current form has 30 fields and checkboxes, cut it to 7 questions and open text. Watch what happens to the quality of answers. And the quality of your first sessions.

## FAQ

### How many questions should an intake form have?

5-8 open-ended questions is the sweet spot. Research shows completion rates drop 15-20% for every additional 5 questions beyond 10. Seven questions takes about 10 minutes to complete and gives you enough depth to prepare for session one.

### What types of questions work best?

Open text fields, not checkboxes. Checkboxes collect data. Open fields collect stories. Ask questions like "What made you decide to start coaching right now?" and "What does success look like in 90 days?" These force specific, thoughtful answers.

### When should I send the intake form?

Within 1-2 hours of the client signing up. Clients are most engaged immediately after the decision. The intake form is part of the first 48-hour onboarding sequence — not a standalone task to send "when you have time."

### What do I do with the answers?

Three steps: (1) Review responses before the first session — not during. (2) Send a brief personalized prep note referencing specific answers. (3) Store responses with the client profile for reference throughout the engagement. The form is only as valuable as what happens after submission.

### Should I customize the intake form per package type?

Same core questions, slight language adjustments. A 12-week executive coaching client gets slightly different framing than a 4-week career transition client. But the 7 core questions work universally. Don't over-complicate it.
