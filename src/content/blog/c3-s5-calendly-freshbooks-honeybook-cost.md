# Calendly + FreshBooks + HoneyBook + Mailchimp: what you're actually paying for

I talk to coaches and consultants every week who run some version of the same stack. Calendly for scheduling. FreshBooks for invoicing. HoneyBook for contracts and client management. Mailchimp for email. The Calendly FreshBooks HoneyBook Mailchimp cost combined starts at $115/mo. That is the number on the invoices. The actual cost is higher.

This is a breakdown of what each tool charges, what you realistically use, and where your time goes filling the gaps between them. If you have not run a [subscription audit](/blog/tool-consolidation/saas-subscription-audit) recently, this will be a useful starting point.

---

## The sticker price

Here is what the standard [coaching tool stack](/blog/coaching-automation/coaching-tool-stack) costs at the most common tier for a solo consultant or small coaching practice:

| Tool | Plan | Monthly Cost | What It Covers |
|---|---|---|---|
| Calendly | Teams | $16/user/mo | Scheduling, round-robin, integrations |
| FreshBooks | Plus | $30/mo | Invoicing, expenses, up to 50 clients |
| HoneyBook | Essentials | $59/mo | Contracts, proposals, client portal |
| Mailchimp | Standard | $20/mo | Email campaigns, automations, 500 contacts |
| **Total** | | **$125/mo** | |

That $125 assumes one Calendly user, 500 or fewer Mailchimp contacts, and fewer than 50 FreshBooks clients. Scale any of those and the number climbs fast. Mailchimp Standard jumps to $100+ at 5,000 contacts. FreshBooks Premium is $55/mo once you pass 50 clients. HoneyBook Essentials recently repriced from $39 to $59/mo.

A more realistic number for an established consultant is $146 to $200/mo.

---

## What you pay for vs. what you use

This is the part that matters more than the sticker price. Pendo's research shows 80 percent of SaaS features are rarely or never used. That tracks with what I see in every stack audit.

### Calendly: $16/mo

**What you pay for:** Team scheduling, round-robin routing, collective meetings, custom branding, integrations with Salesforce and HubSpot, SMS notifications, analytics dashboard.

**What you actually use:** A booking link. One or two meeting types. Maybe the Google Calendar sync.

**What is still manual:** After someone books, you still have to check who they are. Pull up their history. Look at previous notes. Send any pre-meeting materials. Calendly knows someone booked. It does not know anything about them.

### FreshBooks: $30/mo

**What you pay for:** Invoicing, expense tracking, time tracking, financial reports, bank connections, late payment reminders, client portal, project profitability.

**What you actually use:** Creating invoices. Checking who paid. Maybe sending one reminder.

**What is still manual:** Someone signs a contract in HoneyBook. You go to FreshBooks and create the invoice manually. Someone finishes a coaching session. You go create another invoice manually. The two systems do not know about each other.

### HoneyBook: $59/mo

**What you pay for:** Proposals, contracts, invoicing (yes, duplicative with FreshBooks), client portal, project management, automations, lead pipeline, scheduling (yes, duplicative with Calendly), branding.

**What you actually use:** Contracts. Intake forms. Maybe the proposal template.

**What is still manual:** After a client signs a contract, you manually add them to your client tracker (probably a Google Sheet), create their invoice in FreshBooks, add them to your Mailchimp list, and set up their onboarding tasks in whatever project tool you use. One signature triggers four manual steps in four different tools.

### Mailchimp: $20/mo

**What you pay for:** Email campaigns, automations, landing pages, audience segmentation, A/B testing, social posting, content studio, analytics, customer journeys.

**What you actually use:** Sending a newsletter. Maybe one welcome sequence.

**What is still manual:** Keeping the list accurate. Someone becomes a client in HoneyBook but stays in your "prospect" segment in Mailchimp until you manually move them. Someone has not opened an email in six months but you would not know unless you specifically check Mailchimp analytics, which you do not.

## The real cost: the gaps

Add it up. You are paying $125 to $200/mo for four tools. You use roughly 20 percent of each one. And the tasks that matter most, the handoffs between booking and invoicing, between contracts and onboarding, between client activity and email segmentation, are all manual.

The Lokalise workplace study found that workers switch between apps 33 times per day. For a consultant running this stack, that number feels low. Every new client is a tour through four dashboards, copying information from one into another, hoping nothing falls through.

Here is the cost nobody puts on a spreadsheet:

| Hidden Cost | Time Per Week | Monthly Impact |
|---|---|---|
| Manual invoice creation after sessions | 2 hrs | 8 hrs |
| Copying client data between tools | 1.5 hrs | 6 hrs |
| Checking payment status across platforms | 1 hr | 4 hrs |
| Email list maintenance | 1 hr | 4 hrs |
| Onboarding admin per new client | 45 min per client | Varies |
| **Total admin overhead** | **~6 hrs/week** | **~24 hrs/month** |

Six hours a week. That is almost a full working day, every week, spent being the glue between tools that were each supposed to save you time.

## What consolidation actually changes

The point is not that Arca is cheaper. At $97/mo for Foundation, it is. But cheaper tools are not hard to find. Cheaper tools that still do not talk to each other solve nothing.

What changes is the gaps disappear. Someone books. The system already knows who they are, what they bought, when they last paid, and what to do next. No copy-pasting. No switching tabs. No remembering to do the thing after the other thing.

One consultant I worked with described it like this: "I stopped managing my tools and started managing my clients."

If you want to see this in practice, read the [migration story](/blog/tool-consolidation/seven-tools-to-one-migration) of a consultant who replaced all seven tools in seven days. Or start with the [full stack replacement guide](/blog/tool-consolidation/replace-saas-stack-guide) if you want the strategic picture first.

The four tools in this stack are not bad products. They are good products that were never designed to work together. And you are spending $125/mo plus 24 hours/month making up the difference yourself.
