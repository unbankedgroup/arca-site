# How Much Time Are You Actually Spending on Admin? A Coaching Business Time Audit

I asked a life coach how many hours a week she spent on admin. She said five, maybe six.

Then I asked her to track it. One week. Every task that wasn't coaching a client.

The number was 19 hours.

That's not unusual. Most coaches dramatically underestimate how many admin hours they actually work. A coaching business time audit almost always reveals the same thing: the admin isn't a sliver of your week. It's nearly half of it.

The industry data backs this up. Coaches report spending 20-30 hours a week on non-coaching work. Scheduling. Rescheduling. Invoicing. Chasing payments. Sending intake forms. Writing follow-up emails. Updating spreadsheets. Switching between tabs.

Research from the International Coaching Federation found that 41% of solopreneurs cite time management as their single biggest challenge, ahead of marketing, cash flow, and everything else. A separate study of 500+ coaching businesses showed the average founder spends 18.7 hours per week on administrative tasks — nearly half of a standard work week.

The reason the number surprises people is that admin doesn't feel like work. It feels like the stuff between the work. Five minutes here. Ten minutes there. A quick email after a session. A calendar check before bed. None of it registers as a task because none of it happens in a block. It's scattered across the entire day in fragments.

But fragments add up.

## How to run your own time audit

You don't need a fancy tool for this. A notes app works. Track one full work week. Every time you do something that isn't directly coaching a client, write it down with a rough time estimate.

Here are the categories to watch.

**Scheduling and rescheduling.** Every time you send a calendar link. Every time a client asks to move their session. Every back-and-forth about availability. Every time you manually add something to your calendar. This alone eats 2-4 hours a week for most coaches.

**Invoicing and payments.** Creating invoices. Sending them. Checking if they've been paid. Following up on the ones that haven't. Reconciling what came in with what was owed. For coaches with 10+ clients, this is another 2-3 hours weekly.

**Client communication.** Session reminders. Pre-session prep emails. Post-session follow-ups. Check-ins between sessions. Welcome emails for new clients. Each one takes 5-10 minutes. Multiply by 15-20 clients. That's 3-5 hours a week you never see on a to-do list.

**Onboarding new clients.** Sending the welcome packet. Getting the intake form filled out. Collecting payment details. Scheduling the first session. Walking them through what to expect. A proper [onboarding flow](/blog/coaching-automation/coaching-client-onboarding) takes 30-60 minutes per new client. If you're taking on 2-3 new clients a month, that's another hour or two each week averaged out.

**Lead follow-up.** Responding to inquiries. Booking discovery calls. Following up after discovery calls. Sending proposals or packages. [Leads that go cold](/blog/coaching-automation/coaching-leads-going-cold) represent both time wasted and revenue lost. This is 1-3 hours a week depending on your pipeline.

**Tool management.** Logging into different platforms. Checking dashboards. Updating your CRM. Exporting data from one tool to import into another. Troubleshooting when an integration breaks. The average small business uses 20+ digital tools. Every switch between tools costs focus and time.

## The benchmarks

After working with coaches across different specialties, here's what a typical time audit reveals.

| Admin category | Hours per week |
|---|---|
| Scheduling and rescheduling | 2-4 |
| Invoicing and payments | 2-3 |
| Client communication | 3-5 |
| Onboarding | 1-2 |
| Lead follow-up | 1-3 |
| Tool management | 1-2 |
| **Total** | **10-19** |

The lower end is coaches with fewer than 10 clients and simple service offerings. The upper end is coaches with 15-25 clients, group programs, or multiple service tiers.

If you're at 15+ hours a week, you're spending roughly a third of your work week on tasks that aren't coaching. That's one or two full days every week that could be sessions, or rest, or business development, or just life outside work.

## What the hours actually cost

Time has a dollar value. If you bill $150/hour and spend 15 hours a week on admin, that's $2,250 in revenue you can't access. Every week. $9,000 a month.

You're not spending that money. You're just not earning it. Because the hours are going to scheduling emails and invoice reminders instead of client sessions.

Some coaches push back on this math. "I wouldn't fill all those hours with clients even if I had them." Fair. But would you fill some of them? Could you take on two more clients a week if you had the time? That's $1,200-2,000 a month in new revenue. From time you already have but can't use.

And there's a cost beyond revenue. 67% of coaches experience severe burnout in their first three years. 43% consider quitting entirely. Not because coaching is hard. Because [running the business around the coaching](/blog/coaching-automation/coaching-business-automation-guide) is hard.

The Harvard Business Review reports that knowledge workers switch between apps 1,100 times per day on average, with task switching costing up to 40% of productive time. For coaches juggling five or more disconnected tools, that cognitive overhead accounts for 6-8 hours of lost capacity every week.

The admin hours aren't just expensive. They're exhausting. The mental weight of remembering who needs a follow-up, which invoice is overdue, and when the next session is. That background processing runs all day, even when you're not at your desk.

## What to do with the results

Once you have your numbers, you're looking at two categories.

**Tasks that require your judgment.** Discovery calls. Session prep where you're reviewing client progress. Personalized check-ins that reference specific conversations. Writing proposals for complex engagements. These need you. They're worth your time.

**Tasks that are the same every time.** Session reminders. Invoice generation. Payment follow-ups. Welcome emails. Scheduling confirmations. Onboarding sequences. These follow the same pattern, same timing, same message structure for every client. They're repeatable.

Most coaches find that 70-80% of their admin falls into the second category. The same steps, triggered by the same events, producing the same outputs. Every time.

Those are the hours you get back first. Not by working faster. Not by hiring someone to do them for you (though that's one option). By building operational infrastructure that handles the repeatable parts automatically.

Run the audit. See the real number. Then decide if that's how you want to spend your week.

## FAQ

### How do I track my admin time accurately?

Use a simple notes app or spreadsheet for one week. Every time you do something that isn't coaching a client, write it down with a rough time estimate. Don't try to be precise — round numbers work. The goal is to reveal patterns, not billable hours.

### What counts as "admin" vs. "coaching work"?

Admin is anything that supports the business but doesn't involve direct client interaction. Scheduling, invoicing, follow-ups, onboarding, tool management, and email all count. Session prep is a gray area — if you're reviewing client progress, that's coaching work. If you're hunting for their intake form, that's admin.

### Is 15-20 hours of admin per week normal?

Yes, unfortunately. Industry data shows coaches spend 20-30 hours weekly on non-coaching tasks. The range depends on client count and how much of your operations are automated. Below 10 hours is rare without dedicated support staff.

### What's the first admin task I should automate?

Start with the task you do most often that follows the same pattern every time. For most coaches, that's session reminders, invoice generation, or lead follow-up. Pick one. Automate it. Then move to the next.

### Can I recover all 15-20 hours through automation?

Not all of them. Some tasks require your judgment — discovery calls, personalized check-ins, complex proposals. But 70-80% of admin is repeatable and can be automated. That's 10-15 hours per week back. Enough to take on 2-3 more clients or actually take a weekend off.
