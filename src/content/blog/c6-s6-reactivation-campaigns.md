# Reactivation Campaigns: Bringing Cold Leads Back to Life

**Quick answer:** Reactivation campaigns are automated sequences sent to leads who went cold or clients who haven't engaged in 90+ days. A well-structured reactivation sequence recovers 15-25% of dormant leads — revenue you've already earned but never collected.

---

I was looking at a coach's pipeline last quarter. She had 147 leads in her CRM. 23 had become clients. The rest were marked "not interested," "timing bad," or just... silent.

She was about to start a new ad campaign. Spend $3K to get fresh leads. I told her to stop.

Her reactivation campaign — three emails sent to those 124 cold leads — generated $18,000 in two weeks. No ad spend. No new traffic. Just revenue that was already hers, waiting to be collected.

Reactivation campaigns are the lowest-hanging fruit in your business. These people already know you. They already expressed interest. Something stopped them from buying. Your job is to find out what — and fix it.

## Who to reactivate

There are three audiences for reactivation.

### 1. Cold leads (90+ days since last contact)

These people inquired, had a discovery call, or downloaded something — then went silent.

**Why they went cold:**
- Timing was wrong (budget cycle, life event, priority shift)
- They bought from a competitor
- They got busy and forgot
- They had an objection they didn't voice

**Reactivation potential:** High. 15-25% recovery rate is common.

### 2. Dormant clients (6+ months since last engagement)

These people worked with you once. They got value. They just never came back.

**Why they went dormant:**
- They achieved their goal (and have a new one)
- They had a life interruption
- They assumed you were "full"
- They forgot you existed

**Reactivation potential:** Very high. 25-40% re-engagement rate. They already trust you.

### 3. Cart abandoners (started signup, didn't finish)

These people got to your checkout or application page — then stopped.

**Why they abandoned:**
- Sticker shock
- Technical issue
- Got interrupted
- Needed approval from someone else

**Reactivation potential:** Medium-high. 10-20% recovery rate.

## The reactivation sequence structure

Here's the framework that works.

### Email 1: The "checking in" (Day 0)

**Subject line options:**
- "Still thinking about it?"
- "Checking in"
- "Quick question"
- "Are you still interested in [outcome]?"

**Body:**
```
Hi [Name],

I was reviewing my calendar and thought of you. You had reached out about [their goal] a few months ago.

Are you still working on that? Or did you find a solution?

No pressure either way. Just checking in.

[Your name]
```

**Why it works:**
- Casual, not salesy
- Assumes they may have solved it (not pushy)
- Easy to reply to
- No link to click (doesn't look like a pitch)

### Email 2: The "new information" (Day 3-5)

If they don't reply to Email 1, send new information.

**Subject line:**
- "Quick update"
- "Something that made me think of you"
- "[Mutual contact] asked about you"

**Body:**
```
Hi [Name],

I just wrapped up a [session/cohort/case study] that made me think of you.

[One specific insight or result that's relevant to their goal]

It reminded me of what you were working on. If you're still pursuing [goal], I have [one spot / a cohort starting / a resource] that might help.

Want me to send details?

[Your name]
```

**Why it works:**
- Provides value first (not just "buy from me")
- Specific and relevant
- Low-commitment ask

### Email 3: The "breakup" (Day 7-10)

This is your last email. Make it count.

**Subject line:**
- "Last try"
- "Should I close your file?"
- "Permission to let go?"

**Body:**
```
Hi [Name],

I've reached out a couple times about [goal]. Haven't heard back.

Two options:
1. You're all set — awesome. I'll stop bothering you.
2. Something's in the way. If you want to talk about it, I'm here.

Either way, I wish you the best.

[Your name]
```

**Why it works:**
- Gives them an out (removes pressure)
- Opens the door for honesty
- Shows you respect their time

## The reactivation call script

If they reply, get them on a call. Here's the script.

**Opening:**
"Great to hear from you. Last we talked, you were working on [goal]. Where are you at with that now?"

**Listen. Then:**
"What changed since we last talked?"

This question reveals everything. Did they try something else? Did their situation change? What's the real objection?

**If they say "I went with someone else":**
"How's that working out for you?"

If they're unhappy, you have an opening. If they're happy, wish them well and move on.

**If they say "I got busy / timing was bad":**
"Totally get that. Is now a better time to revisit it?"

**If they say "It was too expensive":**
"Understood. Has your budget situation changed? Or is it more about the investment feeling right for where you are?"

Don't discount. Don't negotiate against yourself. Find out if it's real budget or perceived value.

**Closing:**
"Based on what you've told me, I think we can help. Here's what I'd recommend..."

## Automating reactivation

You don't need to remember who to reactivate. Your system should do it.

**Automation triggers:**
- Lead status = "cold" for 90+ days → add to reactivation sequence
- Client last session = 6+ months ago → send "checking in" email
- Cart abandoned = 7+ days → send "forgot something?" email

**Automation sequence:**
1. Tag lead as "cold" after 90 days of silence
2. Add to reactivation list
3. Send Email 1
4. If reply → notify you, remove from sequence
5. If no reply → send Email 2 after 3 days
6. If no reply → send Email 3 after 7 days
7. If no reply → mark as "unresponsive" and stop

This runs in the background. Every quarter, your system reactivates your entire cold list automatically.

## The numbers

Here's what to expect.

**Cold leads (90+ days):**
- 40-50% will reply
- 15-25% will book a call
- 5-10% will become clients

**Dormant clients (6+ months):**
- 50-60% will reply
- 25-40% will re-engage
- 15-25% will buy again

**Cart abandoners (7+ days):**
- 20-30% will reply
- 10-20% will complete signup

For a coach with 100 cold leads, that's 15-25 reactivations. At $3,000 average client value, that's $45,000-75,000 in recovered revenue.

From one campaign. That you automate once.

## When reactivation doesn't work

Some people won't come back. That's fine.

**Don't reactivate:**
- People who had a bad experience (they told you so)
- People who asked to be removed from your list
- People who were abusive or unreasonable
- People who owe you money (collect first, then maybe reactivate)

**Do reactivate:**
- Everyone else

The default should be to reactivate. Most people went cold for innocent reasons. They got busy. Their priorities shifted. They forgot.

Your reactivation campaign is a service. You're reminding them of something they wanted. That's helpful, not annoying.

## The bottom line

Your cold leads are revenue waiting to be collected. Not with ads. Not with content. With a simple sequence that says: "Hey, I remember you. Are you still working on this?"

Automate it. Run it quarterly. Watch 15-25% of your "lost" revenue come back.

## FAQ

### How often should I run reactivation campaigns?

Quarterly is ideal. Monthly is too aggressive. Give people time to have their situation change. Run a campaign every 90 days to catch leads who've gone cold since the last run.

### What if they say no?

Respect it. Mark them as "not interested" and don't contact them again about this offer. You can still send them your newsletter or general updates if they're subscribed.

### Should I offer a discount to reactivate?

Only if price was the stated objection. If they went cold for other reasons (timing, fit, priority), a discount doesn't solve the problem. Ask why they hesitated first.

### Can I reactivate by phone?

Yes, but email is better for initial contact. Phone is higher pressure. Email lets them reply when they're ready. If they engage via email, then you can offer a call.

### What's the best subject line for reactivation?

"Still thinking about it?" or "Checking in" perform best. Short, casual, non-salesy. Avoid "Special offer" or "Discount" — those attract bargain hunters, not committed clients.
